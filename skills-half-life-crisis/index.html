The Skills Half-Life Crisis

Why your skill taxonomy is depreciating — and the three questions that turn a static skills map into a living portfolio.

Your organisation spent eighteen months building a skills taxonomy. You mapped competencies across departments, assigned proficiency levels, created development paths. The spreadsheet is thorough. The board presentation looked sharp. The taxonomy itself? It's a photograph of last year.

While you were mapping, AI compressed the half-life of execution skills from years to months. The skills your taxonomy values most are the ones losing market value fastest. And nobody's tracking the exchange rate.

That translates to a development budget that's overexposed to depreciating assets. Not because your team made a mistake, but because the rules changed mid-game.

The snapshot problem

Most skills frameworks share the same structural flaw. They're static documents trying to describe a moving target.

Think about how your organisation builds its skills map. You convene workshops. You gather input from department heads. You align to industry frameworks. You debate whether "stakeholder management" sits under leadership or communication. The result is a taxonomy that accurately captures what your people can do right now.

The trouble is: "right now" has a shelf life.

The World Economic Forum estimates that 39% of existing skillsets will be transformed or become outdated within five years.1 That's the macro number. For roles with high AI exposure, the window is tighter. Anthropic's Economic Index shows that AI accelerates college-level cognitive tasks by 12x, compared to 9x for simpler work.2 Read that again. The more complex the intellectual work, the faster AI compresses it. The skills your most experienced, most expensive people hold are the ones depreciating most aggressively.

-20%

Skill decline in 90 days

A study published in The Lancet found that gastroenterologists' adenoma detection rate dropped from 28% to 22% after just three months of AI-assisted work. A 20% relative skill decline. In ninety days.3

Your skills map doesn't show this. It shows proficiency levels from last quarter's assessment. The gap between the map and reality is growing every month.

Not all skills depreciate the same way

The standard "skills are important" conversation treats all skills as one category. They're not. Skills behave like different asset classes, with different risk profiles and different trajectories under AI pressure.

The Skills Depreciation Curve

Execution Skills
6–18 months

Report writing, data entry, scheduling, basic analysis

Application Skills
2–4 years

Project management, process design, technical implementation

Direction Skills
5+ years (appreciating)

Strategic judgment, stakeholder navigation, creative synthesis

Bar = depreciation speed. Longer bar = faster depreciation under AI pressure.

Execution skills are tasks where the primary value is getting something done accurately and efficiently. Report writing. Data entry. Content formatting. Scheduling. Basic analysis. These skills had half-lives measured in years. AI is compressing that window to months. For a growing number of these tasks, AI already matches or exceeds trained human performance. Investing heavily in execution skill development is like holding cash in a high-inflation economy. It feels safe. It's losing value every quarter.

Application skills sit in the middle. Project management, process design, system configuration, technical implementation. AI doesn't replace these outright, but it compresses the routine portions. The project manager who spends most of their time on status tracking and reporting will find AI absorbing that work within two years. The strategic half of the role survives. The administrative half doesn't. Call it a half-life of two to four years, and shrinking.

Direction skills are where things get interesting. Strategic judgment. Stakeholder navigation. Ethical reasoning. Creative synthesis. Pattern recognition across domains. These don't depreciate under AI pressure. They appreciate. AI makes direction skills more valuable because it handles the execution layer underneath, freeing the human to focus entirely on the questions that matter: What should we build? For whom? Why?

"The skill of the future is direction and synthesis: it is not execution, the machine executes, you direct."

— Brian Roemmele, You Have 5000 Days4

The question for any CHRO: look at your development budget. What percentage goes to execution skills versus direction skills? If most of your spend trains people on tasks that AI will handle within two years, you're overexposed to the fastest-depreciating category.

The category error

This isn't a failure of effort. It's a failure of classification.

Most organisations invested in skills development without differentiating between asset classes. Every skill got the same treatment: identify the gap, build the training, measure the completion. The logic made sense in a world where skill half-lives were measured in decades, where a competency in financial analysis stayed relevant for an entire career. That world is gone.

The category error shows up in three places.

Overinvestment in execution skills

These are the easiest to train, certify, and count. "428 employees completed the data analysis module" is a clean metric. It's also a depreciating one. If AI absorbs the majority of standard data analysis by the time you've certified employee number 428, the return on that investment is a fraction of what you projected.

Underinvestment in direction skills

Judgment, synthesis, the ability to read a room and change a strategy on the fly. These are hard to define, harder to train, and uncomfortable to measure. So they get pushed down the priority list. But they're the only skills that gain value as AI gets better.

Zero investment in learning velocity

The speed at which people can pick up new skills, drop old ones, and adapt to changing conditions. It doesn't appear in any taxonomy because it's not a skill in the traditional sense. It's a meta-capability. And it's the only one that's truly inflation-proof.

"De-skilling as elevation — AI compresses the execution phase of work. This allows humans to stop being operators and become directors."

— Brian Roemmele, You Have 5000 Days: Consider Phlebas5

That's accurate. But it only works if your development system prepares people for the director role. Right now, most systems are still optimising for operators.

From skills map to skills trajectory map

A traditional skills map answers one question: What can our people do today?

That's necessary. It's also insufficient. A skills trajectory map adds three more questions.

1

What's the half-life?

For every skill in your taxonomy, assign a depreciation estimate. Rapid (6–18 months): AI handles this now or will soon. Moderate (2–5 years): AI is compressing the routine portions. Durable (5+ years): AI amplifies rather than replaces this skill. The labels are less important than the exercise. Once you classify your skills by durability, you'll see where the exposure is concentrated.

2

What's the transformation path?

Skills don't just disappear. They evolve. Report writing becomes narrative strategy. Training delivery becomes learning experience design. Compliance tracking becomes risk pattern recognition. Content production becomes content curation and quality judgment. Map the transformation, not just the loss. This tells your people where they're heading, not just what's fading behind them.

3

How fast is the gap moving?

A static gap between current skills and required skills is manageable. An accelerating gap is a different problem entirely. If the distance between what your people can do and what the market requires is growing faster each quarter, your standard development approach won't keep pace. You need to track gap velocity, not just gap size.

Gap velocity in action

AI sustains complex tasks for ~19 hours with human direction, vs ~2 hours autonomously. That 19-hour number is the value of human executive function. But the 2-hour number is climbing every quarter. That's velocity.2

The board conversation nobody is having

Most CHROs report to the board about skills like this:

Static view

"We've mapped 340 skills across 12 departments. 78% of our workforce is at or above proficiency level. We're investing in closing the remaining gaps."

Trajectory view

"45% of our taxonomy falls in the rapid-depreciation zone. We've redirected 30% of investment from execution training toward direction skills. Gap velocity is decelerating for the first time in three quarters."

The difference isn't cosmetic. The first version treats skills as a stockroom to fill. The second treats them as a portfolio to manage. CFOs understand portfolios. They understand depreciation. They understand rebalancing. This is how L&D earns the language of the boardroom: not by learning to talk about "strategic alignment," but by actually managing human capital the way the business manages every other asset.

What to do this quarter

None of this requires a new platform or a twelve-month programme.

01

Audit the portfolio

Sort every skill into three buckets: execution, application, direction. Calculate what percentage of your development budget goes to each. If execution skills dominate the spend, you're overexposed. Start the rebalance.

02

Add a depreciation review to your quarterly cadence

Every quarter, ask: which skills have lost market value since the last review? Which have gained? This is the skills equivalent of an impairment test. Every company does this for financial assets. Almost none do it for human capital.

03

Invest in learning velocity

Build learning systems that reward adaptation, not accumulation. Measure how fast people can ramp into new skill areas, not just whether they completed a module.

"Those who start marshaling AI now will be so far ahead in five years it won't even be funny. Their skills will compound."

— Brian Roemmele, You Have 5000 Days: Everyone Is Doing It6

He's right. But the compounding only works if you're compounding the right asset class. Direction skills compound. Execution skills depreciate. The difference matters.

The currency still holds

Skills are still the currency of adaptation. That hasn't changed. What's changed is the exchange rate.

Some skills are inflating. Some are appreciating. The organisations that can tell the difference, and invest accordingly, build the only advantage that holds. Your skills map isn't wrong. It's incomplete. Add the trajectory layer. Track the depreciation. Rebalance the portfolio.

Stop measuring what your people know today. Start measuring how fast they can learn what matters tomorrow.

A skills map without a depreciation layer is a balance sheet without liabilities. It looks healthy until the audit.

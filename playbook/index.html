Nearly half of CEOs—people who've built successful businesses—are questioning whether their organizations can survive the next ten years on their current trajectory.

The Skill Maturity Model

Where Are You Today?

Most organizations operate at Stage 1 or 2. The problem isn't effort—it's infrastructure. You can't manage what you can't see, and most companies have no visibility into the skills they have, need, or are developing.

1
Ad-Hoc

Training happens, but there's no systematic view of skills across the organization. Development is reactive—driven by individual requests or compliance requirements. No one can answer: "What skills do we have? What do we need?"

Signs you're here: Spreadsheet tracking, no skill inventory, training driven by catalog availability
2
Tracked

A learning management system exists. Completion rates are measured. But there's no connection between training consumption and actual skill development—or between skills and business outcomes. You know who took what course, but not whether it mattered.

Signs you're here: LMS in place, completion reports, no skill gap analysis, no business impact data
3
Measured

Skills are defined, mapped to roles, and tracked over time. Gap analysis is possible. You can identify which teams have capability shortfalls and measure whether development activities are closing gaps. Beginning to connect learning to performance.

Signs you're here: Skill taxonomy exists, gap analysis possible, manager visibility, development paths defined
4
Strategic

Skills are connected to business outcomes. L&D has a seat at the strategy table because it can demonstrate ROI in business terms. The CFO sees skill development as an asset, not a cost. Capability planning informs workforce strategy.

Signs you're here: ROI calculations, CFO-ready reports, strategic conversations, capability as competitive advantage

The Skill-Based Operating Model

Moving from Stage 2 to Stage 3 requires four interconnected elements. None is sufficient alone; together, they create the infrastructure for strategic skill management.

  • Skills Taxonomy

    A shared vocabulary for capabilities across the organization. Start with 20-30 core skills that matter most to your strategy. Don't try to boil the ocean—you can expand later. The goal is consensus on what matters, not completeness.

  • Skill Mapping

    Connect skills to roles, teams, and business priorities. Which roles require which skills at what level? This creates the foundation for gap analysis and targeted development. Without mapping, you're developing skills randomly.

  • Gap Analysis

    Compare required skills to current capabilities. Identify where the biggest gaps are—by team, by skill, by business priority. This tells you where to focus development resources for maximum impact.

  • Development Paths

    Clear routes from current capability to target proficiency. Not just "take this course" but "here's how you grow from level 2 to level 4 in this skill." Make development visible and achievable.

The Minimum Viable Skill System

You don't need a perfect taxonomy or complete mapping to start. You need:

  • → 20-30 core skills defined (start with your strategy's most critical capabilities)
  • → 3-5 priority roles mapped (focus on where gaps hurt most)
  • → Current state assessed (even a rough baseline beats no baseline)
  • → Top 3 gaps identified (you can't fix everything—pick the most impactful)

The 90-Day Implementation

You don't need six months or a massive transformation program. You need 90 focused days to build the foundation for strategic skill management. This roadmap takes you from wherever you are today to measurable capability and a defensible business case.

Days 1-30

Foundation

  • Identify top 3 gaps
  • Map to business priorities

    Activation

    Measurement

    By Day 90: You have a working skill taxonomy, visible gaps, tracked development, early ROI evidence, and a business case for continued investment. That's the foundation for Stage 3-4 maturity.

    12

    Days 1-30: Building the Foundation

    The first month is about creating visibility. You can't manage skills you can't see, and you can't improve what you can't measure. Focus on getting the basics right—speed beats perfection.

    Week 1-2: Define Your Skill Taxonomy
    • Review your strategic plan—what capabilities does success require?
    • Interview 3-5 senior leaders: "What skills are you most worried about?"
    • Draft initial list of 20-30 core skills (resist the urge to go bigger)
    • Define 4-5 proficiency levels for each skill (basic descriptors are fine)
    • Get sign-off from one executive sponsor (you need air cover)
    Week 2-3: Run Baseline Assessment
    • Select 2-3 pilot teams for initial assessment (preferably with engaged managers)
    • Use self-assessment plus manager validation (perfect accuracy isn't the goal)
    • Map current skill levels against role requirements
    • Document what you find—gaps and surprises both matter
    Week 3-4: Identify Priority Gaps
    • Analyze assessment data: Where are the biggest gaps?
    • Cross-reference with business priorities: Which gaps hurt most?
    • Select top 3 gaps to address (you can't fix everything at once)
    • Connect each gap to a business outcome (cost, time, quality, risk)
    • Document the baseline for later ROI calculation

    Common Mistake: Trying to create a comprehensive taxonomy before taking action. Start with "good enough" and iterate. A working 20-skill taxonomy beats a perfect 200-skill taxonomy that took 6 months to build.

    13

    Days 31-60: Activating Development

    Month two shifts from assessment to action. You know where the gaps are; now you need to close them—while making progress visible to the people who matter.

    Week 5-6: Launch Targeted Development
    • For each priority gap, identify 2-3 development resources (courses, projects, mentoring)
    • Create clear development paths: "To grow from Level 2 to Level 3, do X, Y, Z"
    • Assign development activities to people with the identified gaps
    • Set expectations with managers: their role is to enable and check in, not police
    • Communicate the "why" to participants—connect to their career, not just compliance
    Week 6-7: Enable Manager Visibility
    • Give managers dashboards showing their team's skill levels and progress
    • Train managers on having skill development conversations (5-minute version)
    • Create a simple cadence: monthly skill check-ins (15 minutes per direct report)
    • Make managers partners, not spectators—their buy-in determines adoption
    Week 7-8: Document Early Wins
    • Track skill level changes (even small improvements matter)
    • Collect qualitative feedback: What's working? What's hard?
    • Identify 2-3 success stories you can share (with permission)
    • Note time savings, efficiency gains, or problems avoided
    • Start building your ROI evidence file
    Why Managers Matter More Than L&D

    Research shows employees are 2x more likely to learn from their managers than from L&D teams. Don't fight this—leverage it. The L&D team's job is to enable managers with visibility and tools, not to compete with them for learning mindshare.

    14

    Days 61-90: Proving the Value

    The final month is about translation—converting what you've built and learned into the business language that earns ongoing investment. This is where you join the 4% who can prove ROI.

    Week 9-10: Calculate ROI Metrics
    • Measure skill level improvements (baseline vs. current)
    • Calculate time to competency for new hires or role transitions
    • Estimate cost avoidance: What would hiring this capability have cost?
    • Document productivity gains or quality improvements (be conservative)
    • Calculate total investment (time, tools, content, opportunity cost)

    Simple ROI Calculation

    ROI = (Value Generated - Investment) / Investment × 100%

    Example: Closing a skill gap that would have required a €80K hire, with €15K in development investment = (€80K - €15K) / €15K = 433% ROI

    Week 11-12: Present and Plan
    • Prepare a 5-slide executive summary: Problem → Approach → Results → ROI → Next Steps
    • Schedule presentation with CEO/CFO (the people who control investment)
    • Lead with business outcomes, not learning metrics
    • Propose scale-up: More teams, more skills, more depth
    • Request resources for sustained program (don't let momentum die)

    The CFO Test: Can you explain your L&D impact in one number that connects to the P&L? If not, keep refining until you can. "We invested X and avoided Y in hiring costs" beats "completion rates increased 40%."

    Calculating ROI Your CFO Will Accept

    The goal is a defensible number that connects skill development to the P&L. Here are three calculation approaches, from simple to comprehensive.

    Approach 1: Cost Avoidance

    Compare internal development cost to external hiring cost for equivalent capability.

    ROI = (Hiring Cost Avoided - Development Investment) / Development Investment

    Example: Developing 5 people to senior level internally (€25K) vs. hiring 5 seniors externally (€150K incl. recruitment, ramp-up) = 500% ROI

    Approach 2: Productivity Gain

    Estimate productivity improvement from skill development, converted to € value.

    ROI = (Productivity Gain × Avg Salary × Headcount - Investment) / Investment

    Example: 10% productivity gain for 50 people earning €60K avg = €300K value. Investment of €40K = 650% ROI

    Approach 3: Turnover Reduction

    Calculate value of reduced turnover attributed to development investment.

    ROI = (Turnover Reduction × Replacement Cost - Investment) / Investment

    Example: 5% turnover reduction on 200 people (10 fewer departures × €30K replacement cost = €300K saved). Investment of €50K = 500% ROI

    CFO Tip: Use conservative estimates and be transparent about assumptions. Under-promising and over-delivering builds credibility faster than optimistic projections that fall short.

    The Dutch Advantage: SLIM Subsidy

    Dutch SMEs have access to government co-investment that reduces the cost and risk of L&D initiatives. The SLIM-regeling (Subsidieregeling Leren en Ontwikkelen in het MKB) is specifically designed to help mid-market companies build capability.

    What SLIM Covers

    • Learning and development activities for employees
    • External training, courses, and skill assessments
    • Development of internal learning infrastructure
    • Career development and mobility support
    • Sustainable employability initiatives
    Company Size Maximum Subsidy Subsidy Rate
    Small (< 50 employees) Up to €25,000 80% of costs
    Medium (50-250 employees) Up to €25,000 60% of costs
    Sector initiatives Up to €500,000 60% of costs
    Using SLIM in Your CFO Conversation

    SLIM subsidy effectively reduces your investment risk by 60-80%. When presenting ROI calculations, show both gross investment and net investment after SLIM. A €40K initiative becomes €16K net investment with 60% subsidy—dramatically improving the ROI calculation.

    Application windows: SLIM has periodic application windows. Check RVO.nl for current deadlines and requirements. Applications are competitive, so prepare early.
